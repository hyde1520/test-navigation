About Landscapes in IFTDSS

The drop-downs below provide brief explanations of working with landscapes in IFTDSS. Landscapes in IFTDSS are represented with landscape (.lcp) files. The current versions of landscape files available in IFTDSS include:

  • LANDFIRE 2012
  • LANDFIRE 2014

Visit the Creating Landscapes Topic for details on how to create a landscape in IFTDSS.

What are Landscape files in IFTDSS?

A Landscape file (.LCP) is a multi-band raster format commonly used by wildland fire behavior and fire effects models such as FARSITE and FlamMap. The bands of an .LCP file store data describing terrain, tree canopy, and fuel model (outlined in the table below). The .LCP file defines your project area and is the first input needed to run most models in IFTDSS. LCP's generated in IFTDSS are derived from LANDFIRE.

Data contained in IFTDSS LCP files:

Band # Band Name Units - GeoTiff Units - IFTDSS (Reports)
1 Elevation meters feet
2 Slope degrees degrees
3 Aspect degrees degrees
4 Fuel Model categorical categorical
5 Canopy Cover % %
6 Stand Height meters * 10 meters
7 Canopy Base Height meters * 10 meters
8 Canopy Bulk Density kg/m3 * 100 kg/m3
9 EVT (Existing Vegetation Type) categorical N/A
10 EVC (Existing Vegetation Cover) categorical N/A
11 EVH (Existing Vegetation Height) categorical N/A
12 Zone (LANDFIRE Map Zone) categorical N/A

Buffering Around Landscapes

Landscapes created in IFTDSS are automatically given a 4980 meter buffer, as seen below outside of the blue boundary. This buffer will be useful when future modeling capabilities are released such as:

  • Minimum Travel Time Fire Spread (MTT Fire Spread) modeling where you may want to simulate an ignition that occurs outside your landscape and burns into it.
  • Modeling Landscape Burn Probability requiring ignitions placed outside of a landscape so that fires can burn onto the landscape. Additionally, fires that start within the landscape must be able to burn into the buffer to avoid erroneous edge effects.

The Landscape Extent, beyond which the boundary extends, is delineated by the blue line when viewing it in Map Studio. This line may be hidden by unselecting it in the Layer List.

Landscapes created in earlier versions of IFTDSS were not assigned a buffer because that feature did not exist yet. These early landscapes also have blue boundary, but lack the surrounding buffer. They are identified throughout IFTDSS by the prefix "NB" at the end of the landscape name, or preceding the file type. "NB" indicates "No Buffer". For example, an old landscape that used to be named "Light Thin Pile Burn All" now appears as "Light Thin Pile Burn All-NB" and is identified as an "NB Landscape" in MyWorkspace.

Generating Reports with Buffered Landscapes

The buffer around your landscape (the area outside the blue landscape boundary) IS NOT included in Summary Report statistics generated in IFTDSS. Only the area inside the landscape boundary is included in reports.

Editing Buffered Landscapes

Edits are applied to your landscape as well as this buffer area, including landscape edits and edits constrained by a mask. However, note that Summary Reports generated for your landscape only include the area inside the blue landscape boundary. If an Area of Interest (AOI) is used in generating a landscape summary, only the region of the AOI that intersects the landscape area is analyzed and included in the report.

About LCP files in IFTDSS

It is important to know that canopy cover, canopy base height, canopy bulk density, stand height in IFTDSS refer to Forest canopy characteristics. If you are working in an area classified as shrub or grass by LANDFIRE, your cover and base height values are zero. However, when you export LCP data from IFTDSS, you have additional layers available including, the Existing Vegetation Cover (EVC) and Existing Vegetation Height (EVH) layers. These layers will reflect cover and height data for tree, shrub, and herb lifeforms.

Choosing Your Landscape Size or Extent

Choosing the appropriate landscape extent for your landscape and understanding how IFTDSS uses landscapes will facilitate your work. Thirty meters is the default resolution of all landscapes extracted within IFTDSS from LANDFIRE. Define a landscape extent that covers your area of interest and immediate surroundings. For individual projects this may be a few thousand acres or less. For unit level analysis, it may be several hundred thousand acres or larger. Understanding how IFTDSS uses landscapes will facilitate more efficient work practices within the application. For instance, paying close attention to choosing the appropriate landscape extent for your project can not only help you focus in on your specific area of interest, but will provide for faster computing times as landscapes are built and models are run.

Landscape Editing Overview

Landscape editing can occur in both the Landscape Evaluation stage of the Planning Cycle under Landscape Edit, or within the Strategic Planning stage under Develop Treatment Alternatives. Menu options and results are the same for both. A key distinction between the two editing options is the landscapes that are available to be edited: In editing sessions accessed from the Landscape Evaluation stage any landscape can be edited. In editing sessions accessed from the Develop Treatment Alternatives task in the Strategic Planning stage, your Originating Landscape (the landscape you choose to specify your editing area for simulating treatment alternatives) must be an unedited landscape. Other landscapes that match the extent of this unedited landscape (ie one you've already edited in the Landscape Evaluation stage) will become available during this process. This limitation on landscape availability is to ensure that you are comparing "like" landscapes and outputs in your comparisons.

For a step by step walk-through of making edits in IFTDSS, see the Landscape Editing topic.

Landscape Terminology in IFTDSS

There are only two types of landscapes in IFTDSS, unedited or edited. However, there are a variety of terms that reference these different landscape types depending on how they are being used. We will outline them below:

Some terms are not mutually exclusive.

Starting Landscape

Starting Landscape: Any landscape chosen as the starting point for a landscape editing session. This term is used for both edited and unedited landscapes, and you will often see it next to the landscape drop-down menu when you begin edits in IFTDSS. In the Landscape Edit task of Landscape Evaluation, any landscape may be chosen as a starting landscape. In the landscape editing portion of the Develop Treatment Alternatives task of Strategic Planning session, the starting landscape choices will be limited to landscapes that match the geographic extent of the Originating landscape (explained below).

Not sure if you're looking at an edited or unedited landscape? You can determine if editing rules have been applied by selecting the landscape in question from My Workspace View and then clicking the view edit rules button.

Extracted Unedited Landscape (LANDFIRE)

Extracted Unedited Landscape (LANDFIRE): Thirty meter (30m) resolution raw “out of the box” landscape data extracted from LANDFIRE that have not yet been edited. This is the landscape that you first create, specifying the size and name. These are the only landscapes available to choose from in Develop Treatment Alternatives when you are prompted to “Pick Landscape”. IFTDSS will always retain this original version under the name you have chosen.

Extracted Unedited Landscape Recommended Naming Convention:

Use a name that reflects geographic extent or Project Area Name eg. Gumdrop Falls State Park, Horse Ridge, etc. Append the name with data source if desired, eg. LFXXXX where XXXX represents the source year. (In the future more versions/years of LANDFIRE data are likely to become available).

An example would be Gumdrop State Park LF2012 or Horse Ridge LF2012

Edited (Alternative) Landscape

Edited (Alternative) Landscape: New landscape saved during a landscape editing session where an edit rule has been applied, such as edits made to create an Existing Condition (EC)(defined below) or to reflect post-treatment conditions that would result from a fuel treatment alternative. You may check to see if editing rules have been applied to a landscape by selecting the landscape in question from My Workspace View and then clicking the view edit rules button.

Edited Landscape Recommended Naming Convention:

Fuels Treatment Alternative Edited landscapes: Append with abbreviated major edits and timeframe Existing Condition Edits landscapes: Append with -EC

Horseshoe Ridge LF2012 Thin 1YR Post

Horseshoe Ridge LF 2012- Low Sev Fire 1 YR Post

Existing Condition (EC) Landscape

Existing Condition (EC) Landscape: A term used specifically in Developing Treatment Alternatives, it refers to a landscape that represents, as closely as possible, the current condition of your landscape. It may be an edited landscape, or unedited. Extracted unedited landscapes can be used to represent EC if you determine the raw LANDFIRE data is current and accurate for your analysis purposes. You will see this term used when comparing treatment alternatives in IFTDSS.

Existing Condition Recommended Naming Convention:

"Horse Ridge LF 2012" (if extracted, unedited LANDFIRE data is used you will not have the opportunity to rename this landscape but you can still uses it to represent the Existing Condition.

"Horse Ridge LF 2012 - EC

Originating Landscape (Zero "0" landscape)

Originating Landscape (Zero “0” landscape): A term used specifically in Developing Treatment Alternatives, it refers to the unedited landscape you select to begin developing treatment alternatives. It will be tagged with a zero icon. It defines the extent of your comparison, so any landscapes that match the extent of your originating landscape will become available during Developing Treatment Alternative task.
