Landscape (.LCP) Files

A Landscape file (.LCP) is a multi-band raster format commonly used by wildland fire behavior and fire effects models such as FARSITE and FlamMap. The bands of an .LCP file store data describing terrain, tree canopy, and fuel model (outlined in the table below). The .LCP file defines your project area and is the first input needed to run most models in IFTDSS. LCP's generated in IFTDSS are derived from LANDFIRE.

Data contained in IFTDSS LCP files:

Band # Band Name Units - GeoTiff Units - IFTDSS (Reports)
1 Elevation meters feet
2 Slope degrees degrees
3 Aspect degrees degrees
4 Fuel Model categorical categorical
5 Canopy Cover % %
6 Stand Height meters * 10 meters
7 Canopy Base Height meters * 10 meters
8 Canopy Bulk Density kg/m3 * 100 kg/m3
9 EVT (Existing Vegetation Type) categorical N/A
10 EVC (Existing Vegetation Cover) categorical N/A
11 EVH (Existing Vegetation Height) categorical N/A
12 Zone (LANDFIRE Map Zone) categorical N/A

Buffering Around Landscapes

Landscapes created in IFTDSS are automatically given a 4980 meter buffer, as seen below outside of the blue boundary. This buffer will be useful when future modeling capabilities are released such as:

  • Minimum Travel Time Fire Spread (MTT Fire Spread) modeling where you may want to simulate an ignition that occurs outside your landscape and burns into it.
  • Modeling Landscape Burn Probability requiring ignitions placed outside of a landscape so that fires can burn onto the landscape. Additionally, fires that start within the landscape must be able to burn into the buffer to avoid erroneous edge effects.

The Landscape Extent, beyond which the boundary extends, is delineated by the blue line when viewing it in Map Studio. This line may be hidden by unselecting it in the Layer List.

Landscapes created in earlier versions of IFTDSS were not assigned a buffer because that feature did not exist yet. These early landscapes also have blue boundary, but lack the surrounding buffer. They are identified throughout IFTDSS by the prefix "NB" at the end of the landscape name, or preceding the file type. "NB" indicates "No Buffer". For example, an old landscape that used to be named "Light Thin Pile Burn All" now appears as "Light Thin Pile Burn All-NB" and is identified as an "NB Landscape" in MyWorkspace.

Generating Reports with Buffered Landscapes

The buffer around your landscape (the area outside the blue landscape boundary) IS NOT included in Summary Report statistics generated in IFTDSS. Only the area inside the landscape boundary is included in reports.

Editing Buffered Landscapes

Edits are applied to your landscape as well as this buffer area, including landscape edits and edits constrained by a mask. However, note that Summary Reports generated for your landscape only include the area inside the blue landscape boundary. If an Area of Interest (AOI) is used in generating a landscape summary, only the region of the AOI that intersects the landscape area is analyzed and included in the report.

About LCP files in IFTDSS

It is important to know that canopy cover, canopy base height, canopy bulk density, stand height in IFTDSS refer to Forest canopy characteristics. If you are working in an area classified as shrub or grass by LANDFIRE, your cover and base height values are zero. However, when you export LCP data from IFTDSS, you have additional layers available including, the Existing Vegetation Cover (EVC) and Existing Vegetation Height (EVH) layers. These layers will reflect cover and height data for tree, shrub, and herb lifeforms.

There can still be confusing cases, as illustrated by the examples below:

In IFTDSS, the stand height for much of this area is listed as zero, why are the trees not shown? Exporting the .LCP and examining it in Arcmap, there is an EVH reporting much of the area as pine barrens. However, the EVT for much of the area is classified as a non-forested fuel type.

Based on the map zone, EVT, and very short tree heights, this area was classified as a shrub fuel type, which makes sense from a fire behavior perspective, but can be confusing from a vegetative perspective.

In IFTDSS, much of the area is mapped as Fuel Model 122, a brush fuel model. However, there are stand heights of 7.5 meters, and in some places 17.5 meters. Zooming into the imagery base layer reveals that the area does indeed have some trees with associated stand heights. However, the brush fuel model appears correct from a fire behavior standpoint.

Editing Considerations

Landscape features are not dependent upon each other when editing. When editing an LCP with User Created Edit Rules, changing one feature such as Fire Behavior Fuel Model, will not make changes to other features such as canopy height cover, etc. For example, changing a Timber Understory Fuel Model to a Grass Fuel Model will change the Fuel Model, but all the timber canopy features will remain until you edit them as well.
