Overview

Fuel consumption is an important variable in fire effects modeling. An understanding of fuel consumption is needed to assess when and how fire should be applied to meet site and landscape objectives and also reduce air quality impacts.

Because Consume captures the inherent complexity of wildland fuels through a close interface with the Fuel Characteristics Classification System, specific fuel strata and/categories can be targeted for prescription, or noted as a potential source of pollutant emissions, depending on the burn scenario.

Consume is applicable to most wildland fires in forests, shrubs, woodlands, and grasslands in North America. Consume includes separate equations for calculating consumption of activity and natural fuels.

The Pacific Northwest Research Station (Sandberg, 1980; Sandberg and Ottmar, 1983; Ottmar et al., 1993), Missoula Fire Lab (Brown et al., 1991; Reinhardt et al., 1997; Albini and Reinhardt, 1997), and the Pacific Southwest Station have been leaders in the development of both theoretical and empirical fuel consumption models. Through that effort, we have a wealth of fuel consumption predictive algorithms for various fuel bed components and combustion phases for prescribed burning in Douglas-fir/western hemlock, west coast hardwoods, and logging slashThe coarse and fine woody debris generated by natural processes (such as wind) or by logging operations.. Development History

In the early 1980s, the Fire and Environmental Resource Application Group (FERAFire and Environmental Research Applications Team. A USDA Forest Service research team focusing on fuels and fire and landscape ecology.) of the Pacific Northwest Research Station began to develop fuel consumption models and emission factors for prescribed burning planning in the Pacific Northwest.

Consume 1.0 included a set of consumption algorithms formulated from data collected at operational broadcast burns in logging slash. As new fuel consumption equations were formulated, Consume 2.1 was developed with algorithms for activity-piled, activity-non-piled, and natural fuelsNatural fuels are fuels that result from natural processes.; and emission factors, making it possible to estimate emissions.

In 2002, FERA received funding from the Joint Fire Science Program (JFSPJoint Fire Science Program. Created by Congress in 1998 to act as an interagency research, development, and applications partnership between the U.S. Department of the Interior and the U.S. Department of Agriculture.) to enhance Consume by adding the ability to predict fuel consumption and smoke emissions in wildland fuelbedsA fuelbed is an area of similar fuel characteristics within a unit. A fuelbed contains one or more fuelbed strata (e.g., canopy, shrubs, etc). (e.g., burn units without recent logging slash).

Consume 3.0 included new consumption algorithms based on recent research on the flaming and smoldering combustion phases in forests and shrublands without recent logging activity in the United States.

During the 1990s, FERA began developing models of fuel consumption by combustion stageA unique burn environment composed of specific types of fuelbed strata. These stages are flaming, smoldering, and residual smoldering. for other fuel types beyond the Pacific Northwest and different configurations of fuels.

In addition, FERA began developing a new system to characterize fuels called the Fuel Characteristic System. WIth this system, managers could select different fuel loadings for input into Consume.
