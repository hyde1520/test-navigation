Fuelbeds

FCCS defines a fuelbed as the inherent physical characteristics of fuels that contribute to fire behavior and effects (Riccardi et al., 2007a). A fuelbed can represent any scale that you consider to be mostly uniform. For example, a 10,000-acreA measure of land used in the Imperial and US systems; 43,560 square feet or 4,840 square yards. One acre is 0.405 hectares. western sagebrush ecosystem may be adequately represented by a single fuelbed, whereas a 100-acre Hawaiian rainforest may require several fuelbeds to capture the variability of fuel characteristics.

Because FCCS fuelbeds are not scale dependent, they are easily mapped, and calculated results can readily be incorporated into geospatial analyses.

Fire Potentials

FCCS calculates FCCS fire potentials, including surface fireA fire that burns only the surface litter and growth of a forest. behavior potential, crown fireA fire that is in the forest canopy. potential, and available fuel potential. The FCCS fire potentials are indexed values, scaled between 0 and 9 and are based on default environmental conditions. They provide a flexible means of expressing any fuelbed as a function of its potential to produce fire behavior or effects. The intent is to provide an approach to quantifying fuels that is easy to communicate among fuels specialists and across a diversity of fuel types. For example, if a ponderosa pine fuelbed located in Idaho has the same fire potentials as a long-leaf pine fuelbed in Florida, then the manager can expect similar fire behavior and fuel consumptionThe amount of fuels by weight (tons/acre) consumed within a specific combustion stage for a fuel category within a fuelbed stratum. under the same environmental conditions.
