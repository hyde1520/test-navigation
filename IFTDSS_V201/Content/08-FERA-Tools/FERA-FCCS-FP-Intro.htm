A web-based software integration framework that organizes and extends previously existing fire and fuels software applications to make fuels treatment planning and analysis more efficient and effective., a fire hazard is an act or phenomenon with the potential to do harm. It can be expressed as potential fire behavior (e.g., fire line intensity, crown scorch height), or a property of the fuels, such as fuel loading or vegetation biomass, or both. A fire hazard is independent of weather and describes the fuels at one point in time.. They can also be used in conjunction with known environmental conditions to predict fire behavior and effects (i.e., scaled downward to reflect fuel moisture conditions, or scaled upward to represent wind speed or slope).

Nine Fire Potentials

FCCS calculates and reports nine FCCS fire potentials for each fuelbed based on benchmark dry fuel conditions (D2L2 dry scenario: 90% foliar moisture of shrubs, 60% foliar moisture of nonwoody fuels, 6% fuel moisture of 1-hr, 7% fuel moisture of 10-hr, and 8% fuel moisture of 100-hr woody fuelsWoody fuels have a true woody stem. Woody fuels include sound wood, rotten wood, and stumps.). These nine FCCS fire potentials are arranged into three summary FCCS fire potentials:

  • available fuel potential, which includes flame available fuel, smoldering available fuel, and residual available fuel potentials
  • crown fire potential, which includes crown fire initiation, crown to crown transmissivity, and crow fire rate of spreadFor FCCS, this is the distance per unit of time of the flaming frontThe zone of a moving fire where the combustion is primarily flaming. Behind this flaming zone, combustion is primarily smoldering. (Adapted from http://www.pbs.org/wgbh/nova/fire/glossary.html.). For other models, the definition might be somewhat different or more specific. potentials
  • surface fire behavior potential, which includes reaction, spread, and flame lengthThe flame length of a spreading surface fire within the flaming front is measured from midway in the active flaming combustion zone to the average tip of the flames. Flame length is an indicator of fire intensity. Flame lengths are divided into classes (low, medium, high, very high).   • moderate surface fire (9.8-ft flames, 13.6 ft/min rate of spread),
  • above-average crowning potential (6 on a scale of 9), and
  • below-average available fuel (30 tons/acre).
