These fuel consumption algorithms were developed to predict fuel consumption and are referred to as the Activity Fuel Consumption Equations. The equations are based on operational broadcast burns in logging slashThe coarse and fine woody debris generated by natural processes (such as wind) or by logging operations. and are semi-empirical to represent a range of burning conditions and fuel characteristics.

Consume uses several different models to calculate consumption of fuels of different sizes.

  • Small (1-hour and 10-hour) fuels are assumed to consume completely in any burn. Consume assumes that all 1-hour and 10-hour fuels in a unit are consumed during a burn, regardless of weather, location, or other conditions. This was determined from data collected in the field. Managers generally do not meet objectives if they burn a unit when only a small portion of the 1-hour and 10-hour fuels are consumed.
  • Medium (100-hour) fuel consumption is calculated based on several factors, including 10-hour fuel moistureMoisture content in fuels determines whether and to what extent fuels will burn. Fuel moisture is a percentage calculated by the weight of contained water in the fuel divided by the ovendry weight of the fuel. content, wind speed, and slopeThe ratio of the elevational change (rise) over a horizontal distance (run).. The equations for 100-hour fuel consumption were derived from fuel consumption theory, with several of the coefficients determined from a burn study (Ottmar et al., 1990) and from fire spread research (Rothermel, 1972).
  • Large (1000-hour to 10000+-hour) fuel consumption is based primarily on the 1000-hour fuel moisture content. Direct measurement of the 1000-hour fuel moisture content is the best predictor of fuel consumption. Other factors include the consumption of 100-hour fuels and duration of ignition time.
