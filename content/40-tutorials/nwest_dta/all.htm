Introduction

Annie is a Fuels Technician who needed to do a hazardous fuels reduction project on the Boise National Forest. Annie used IFTDSS to save time in analysis by developing, modeling, comparing, and generating reports on hazardous fuels reduction treatments within a single interface. Below, she walks us step by step, through her treatment development process from start to finish.

Management Scenario

My name is Annie and I’m a Fuels Technician on the Idaho City Ranger District, Boise Nat’l Forest, about 30 miles northeast of Boise, Idaho. I’ve found IFTDSS to be a useful tool in planning hazardous fuels reduction projects and want to share with you my process on one such project.

Through observation and based on Forest Plan direction, I know we need some sort of fuels reduction treatment in the mixed conifer stands in Granite Creek drainage, approximately 4 miles east of Idaho City. The area hasn’t been treated in decades and the understory and surface fuels could contribute to uncharacteristically intense fire behavior. This would not only pose a hazard to the community of Idaho City and adjacent private land, but also cause undue mortality in this fire-adapted low elevation Ponderosa pine ecosystem that thrives on frequent but low severity fires.

My objectives are to:

  • Reduce surface fuel loading and the overall horizontal and vertical fuelbed continuity to reduce the fire hazard to adjacent private land and the community of Idaho City
  • Return low intensity fire to fire adapted vegetation communities.
  • Locate areas where our actions will be most effective
  • Evaluate what type of treatment will help me achieve these objectives
  • Demonstrate the need for treatment, both quantitatively and spatially, to members of our district interdisciplinary (ID) team.

I turned to IFTDSS for help with this.

Previewing the Landscape

My first stop was in Map Studio to get a good look at the area I’m interested in, and create a landscape to start working from. To do this I: 

  1. Access Map Studio from the top navigation panel, visible on any page in IFTDSS.
  2. Use the Zoom buttons, and mouse, to find my area. The location search box at the top of Map Studio can also be helpful.
  3. Open the Basemap Gallery to get a more detailed baselayer.
  4. Select "Imagery with Labels".

Create the Landscape

Next, I want to create my landscape, which will serve as a starting point for the rest of my analysis. To do this I:

  1. Click on the Landscape Tools button at the bottom of the screen.
  2. Select the Create New tab to create a new landscape.
  3. Select Draw Mode, then drag across the area I’d like to analyze. Alternatively, you can follow along by inputting the coordinates below directly:
    • East: -115.5848
    • West: -115.8096
    • North: 43.8854
    • South: 43.7675
  4. Select the version of LANDFIRE data I want, in this case 2012.
  5. Select the fire behavior fuel model type (13 or 40), here I have chosen Fuel Model 40.
  6. Name my new landscape "Granite Creek LF2012 unedited". I included the name, Landfire version, and its editing/treatment status to make it easy to discern this landscape from others once I start editing.
  7. Choose or create a new folder for this landscape, this is where the landscape will be stored in My Workspace. Here I have created a "Granite Creek Treatments" folder
  8. Once everything looks good, I click the Create button and wait a minute for my landscape to process.

Assessing the Landscape

Identify Tool

I'll assess my landscape in Map Studio, first by:

  1. Using the Landscape Tools button to check the landscape under the "Add to Map" tab, this adds the landscape to the map and the map's Layer List.
  2. After opening the Layer List button , I ensure my landscape is checked, then I can check and uncheck the landscape layers I’d like to view. I’d really like to look at the fuel models to make sure they correspond to what I’m seeing on the ground in this area, so I ensure that box is checked.
  3. I access more information for the landscape and subsequent layers via the small arrows next to the check boxes. In this case, I check the box next to "Fuel Models", then click on the arrow to the left of the checkbox to dropdown to the Fuel Model layer legend.
  4. I click the Identify button in the upper right corner of the page to view more information for each pixel. I select the layer I’d like to view (current landscape), then click on the landscape in an area I’d like to know more about. The "Identify information box" populates with the landscape information for this pixel. For example, I now know that the green-blue pixels on the map correspond to areas of Fuel Model 188 (TL8). I continue examining various areas of interest on my landscape to learn more about what fuel models are present and where. Notice that all of the landscape characteristics come up in the Identify box when you click on the landscape with the button, not just the characteristic you specified in your Layer List.

I’d like to adjust the transparency of my layer so I can see roads, landscape characteristics, and other map features. To do this I click More Options to the right of my Landscape name in Layer List. A dropdown box of options opens. I choose Transparency, which opens a slider bar allowing me to adjust the of transparency level for that layer.

Add reference data

I want to see if there are any

Before I do a full assessment of the landscape characteristics, I’d like to view previous fires that may have occurred in this area. Fortunately, this is easy to do:

  1. Using Add Reference Data button at the bottom of the screen, I see there are many options for additional data layers, including "Ownership" and "Critical Habitat Areas".
  2. For now, I’m only interested in "Disturbance History" – both "Historic Fuel Treatment Polygons", as well as "Historic Wildfire Perimeters". I select both, and the years I’m interested in. These will now appear in my Layer List and on my map.
  3. I use the Identify tool to identify the exact year of the Rabbit Creek and Bannock fires. Then I click the applicable Fire Perimeter layers on and off so I can assess the landscape and fuel models underneath to ensure they’re reflective of these fires.

I follow this same process with the Historic Fuel Treatment Polygons layer as well.

After doing an assessment of all the landscape characteristics (Elevation, Slope, Aspect, Canopy Cover, Stand Height, Canopy Base Height and Canopy Bulk Density) I determine that the Landfire 2012 data in this landscape is reflective of existing conditions, including previous fires. If there had been a more recent disturbance since this 2012 data was produced, such as a fire or fuels treatment I wanted to mimic on this landscape, I could easily create a polygon to reflect that area of interest, then make the appropriate landscape edits to reflect the disturbance. (More information on Landscape Editing is available via the Landscape Editing Topic.

Next, I'll run a summary report and compare these fuel models with potential landscape fire behavior.

Summarizing the Landscape

Creating a Landscape Summary report will run fire behavior under 97th percentile weather conditions, make the outputs available in Map Studio, and summarize behavior and landscape features in a downloadable report with tables and charts. To create the report I:

  1. Click on Planning Cycle in the top navigation.
  2. The cycle opens on the Landscape Evaluation stage by default, where I click the Landscape Summary task.
  3. In Landscape Summary, I click the dropdown box next to Select Landscapes. If the newly created landscape didn’t appear in the drop down, I could use the Refresh button after giving the landscape a short time to process. My landscape appears with a green check next to it (the check indicates it has downloaded completely to my IFTDSS account). From this same screen I'll create an automated report to summarize not only the landscape characteristics, but also the modeled fire behavior outputs specific to this landscape based on approximated worst case (97th percentile fuel moisture and wind) conditions.
  4. I click on Request a Report so my report can begin processing, it’ll take a few minutes. In the meantime, I close the Report dialogue box, then click the View in Map Studio so I can look at the map version of my landscape characteristics. Since I just clicked on Request a Report, 97th percentile modeled fire behavior will be available in Map Studio as well now.

Assessing the map and the report will allow me to learn more about the fuel characteristics and expected fire behavior on my landscape. This informs me where I may place fuels treatments, as well as what kinds of treatments I may need to achieve my objectives. Later, I can use IFTDSS to compare expected fire behavior for various simulated treatments in my project area.

I set up my Layer List to have the Fuel Model checked for the Granite Creek Landscape layer, as well as Flame Lengths for the Auto97th modeled fire behavior output. I can quickly switch between layers to view changes by checking/unchecking the layers, or by using the Swipe Tool.

  1. I click the Swipe Tool.
  2. Next, I specify the layers I want to swipe - the first layer I select will be the layer I view on the top of the slider bar, the second layer checked in Layer List will be the layer on the bottom of the slider bar. With this information, I decide to create two Areas of Interest, or areas on which I’d like to focus my analysis and treatments.

 

Creating Areas of Interest

  1. To create an Area of Interest, or polygon, I change the Basemap Gallery layer to “Imagery with Labels” so I can see landscape imagery.
  2. Then I set up my Layer List to show my 97th percentile fire behavior output layer for Flame Lengths, and set the transparency so I can see roads and topography as I create my Area of Interest, or treatment area.
  3. I select the Create/Edit Shapes button at the bottom of the screen and ensure Polygons is selected in the dropdown box in Create/Edit Shapes.
  4. Then I select Freehand Polygon option, and draw a shape around one of my areas. Next, I select the button again and draw a shape around the second area. I’ve selected two areas in my landscape that exhibited significant fire behavior. I’d like to concentrate on these areas for developing and comparing treatments to meet my objectives of reducing the fire hazard in this area, and returning low intensity fire to this landscape.
  5. To save these two treatments as one shapefile, I click the Copy from Existing Features button , check the Select multipart button option, single left-click one shape, then single left click the other shape, then proceed to naming.
  6. I name this “Granite Creek Proposed Treatment Areas”, then I save this new shape to my existing "Granite Creek Treatments" folder. This will ensure the Area of Interest is filed in the same location in My Workspace as all my other Granite Creek project files.

Compare Alternatives

Once on the Compare Alternatives tab, I select each landscape I want to compare. They will be numbered in the order I select them, and from reading the right hand panel information, I see that this order is very important:

  1. First, I select the edited landscape so it has a "1" next to it. Then, I select the original landscape, so it has a “2” next to it. This order tells IFTDSS to calculate the difference created by our treated landscape (1) on our original landscape (2). For example, if our new landscape has flame lengths of 3 feet, and the original has flame lengths of 7 feet, the difference will be: 3 - 7 = -4. Or in other words, a 4 foot reduction in flame length from the treatment.
  2. Now I want to view comparison outputs on the map and as a report. First, I click the Compare in Summary Report button so that can begin processing, next I click the Compare on Map button to view results on the map. I wait for the map to appear in splitscreen mode, then I click Compare on Map once more, and give the caparison layers a few seconds to download.

Conclusion

I could continue this process and model a more invasive treatment, such as heavier thinning or increased mortality from a more severe broadcast (prescribed) burn, but decide these results will achieve my initial preliminary objectives, and are a good place to start with my project proposal for the District ID Team.

From this process, It’s clear that the expected fire behavior, given 97th percentile fire weather and fuel moisture conditions, in the identified treatment areas is much more intense than what would be considered characteristic in this frequent, but low intensity, fire regime. The reports and maps will demonstrate how these proposed treatments will help achieve the preliminary objectives to reduce surface fuel loading and the overall horizontal and vertical fuelbed continuity, as well as helping to return low intensity fire in these treatment areas.
